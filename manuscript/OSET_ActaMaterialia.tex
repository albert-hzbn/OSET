\begin{abstract}
Classical plasticity takes the dislocation as its irreducible elementary
entity. This choice, powerful for single crystals, fails for grain boundaries,
amorphous solids, nanocrystals near the glass transition, and ceramics, because
the dislocation is a topological object that requires a periodic lattice for its
very definition. We develop the \emph{Oblate Spheroid Excitation Theory}
(\OSET), in which the elementary excitation is a shear-eigenstrained oblate
spheroid (the oblate-spheroidal transformation zone, \OSTZ) embedded in an
elastic continuum. The \OSTZ\ needs no lattice, has a non-singular stress
field, and carries a finite activation energy
$\dF=\half(\beta_1\g^2+\beta_2\eps^2)\,G V_0$ with Eshelby constraint factors
$\beta_1,\beta_2$ fixed by the spheroid aspect ratio. We derive, in full, three
central results: (i) a single \OSTZ\ is, in the far field, a dislocation
\emph{dipole} with an $r^{-3}$ stress field; (ii) a co-planar chain of $N$
\OSTZ s is mathematically identical to a Peierls--Nabarro dislocation of core
width $\zeta=W$ and Burgers vector $b=N\g W$ with a Lorentzian slip-density
profile; and (iii) a full lattice dislocation nucleates at the critical number
$N_c=b_\mathrm{latt}/(\g W)$. From these we obtain the theoretical shear
strength, the Peierls stress, the dislocation core energy, the Frank--Read
stress, and the stacking-fault energy as \emph{derived} quantities. We further
show that a grain-boundary energy is a dislocation-array (Read--Shockley) energy
scaling as $Gb$, \emph{not} a coherency-strain energy, and predict coherent-,
low-, and high-angle boundary energies in seven metals to within a factor of
two. Finally, with no adjustable parameters, \OSET\ reproduces the semi-empirical
constants extracted by Harisankar and Padmanabhan (2025) from 41 superplastic
systems across six material classes: the dilatational eigenstrain to within 2\%,
the shear eigenstrain to within 15\%, and the grain-boundary energy to a
constant median factor of $4.5$. Since dislocations emerge from \OSET\ but
\OSET\ does not require dislocations, \OSET\ is the more fundamental description
of crystalline, nanocrystalline, and amorphous plasticity on a common basis.
The standard (non-novel) algebra --- the Eshelby tensor evaluation, its limiting
cases, the mean-field decoupling, and the numerical-verification suite --- is
collected in the Supporting Information.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The classical dislocation is defined through the Burgers circuit in a Bravais
lattice,
\begin{equation}
\oint_C \mathrm{d}u_i = b_i, \qquad b_i\in\Lambda_\mathrm{Bravais},
\label{eq:burgers}
\end{equation}
and this topological definition carries three unavoidable restrictions.
\textbf{(R1)} It requires a crystal lattice: with no lattice there is no Burgers
vector and no dislocation, so grain boundaries, glasses, metallic liquids and
polymers are excluded by construction. \textbf{(R2)} The Volterra solution has a
singular core, $\sigma\sim Gb/2\pi r$, regularised only by an empirical cutoff
$r_0\sim b$; the core energy is fitted, not derived. \textbf{(R3)} The
elementary entity is athermal --- it carries no intrinsic entropy, so thermal
activation must be added externally through kink-pair or Peierls--Nabarro
overlays.

These limitations are not academic. Grain-boundary sliding (GBS) controls
structural superplasticity, yet general high-angle boundaries possess no
commensurate DSC lattice~\cite{pad-gleiter2012}; nanocrystals below
$d\sim 10b$ exhibit the inverse Hall--Petch effect that lattice plasticity
cannot explain~\cite{trelewicz2007}; and bulk metallic glasses deform plastically
through shear transformation zones (STZs) in the complete absence of
dislocations~\cite{argon1979,falk-langer1998}. A description in which the same
elementary process operates across all of these classes is therefore desirable.

The mesoscopic GBS model of Padmanabhan and co-workers takes precisely such an
elementary process --- the cooperative shear of an oblate-spheroidal volume of
boundary material --- and reproduces the superplastic strain-rate of hundreds
of material conditions~\cite{pad-part1,astanin-part2,sripathi2014,pad-divinski2024,
harisankar2025}. Here we promote that elementary process to a general theory of
plasticity. We replace the lattice-dependent, singular, athermal dislocation
with a geometry-defined, non-singular, thermally activated oblate-spheroidal
excitation (the \OSTZ), and we \emph{derive} the dislocation as its large-$N$
collective limit. \OSET\ requires only three inputs: a length $W$ (the
free-volume correlation length), a shear eigenstrain $\g$ (the strain per
elementary rearrangement), and a shear modulus $G$. Everything else --- Burgers
vectors, Peierls stresses, stacking-fault and grain-boundary energies, hardening
coefficients --- emerges as a derived result.

To keep the main text focused on the new physics, the standard inclusion algebra
on which the theory rests (closed-form evaluation of the Eshelby tensor, its
sphere and thin-disc limits, the mean-field decoupling of the \OSTZ\ Hamiltonian,
and the complete numerical-verification suite) is given in the Supporting
Information; every novel result is derived in full here. Section~\ref{sec:ostz}
defines the \OSTZ\ and derives its activation energy and dipole field;
Section~\ref{sec:emergence} derives the emergence of dislocations;
Section~\ref{sec:derived} the standard dislocation observables;
Section~\ref{sec:gb} the grain-boundary energy; Section~\ref{sec:stat} the
unified rate equation; and Section~\ref{sec:validation} the parameter-free
validation against 41 superplastic systems.

\section{The elementary excitation: the oblate-spheroidal transformation zone}
\label{sec:ostz}

\subsection{Geometry and eigenstrain}
\label{sec:geometry}

The \OSTZ\ is an oblate spheroid of elastic material that has undergone a local
shear eigenstrain. Its semi-axes are
\begin{equation}
a_1=a_2=W\;(\text{glide plane}),\qquad a_3=W/2 \quad\Rightarrow\quad
\alpha\equiv a_3/a_1=\tfrac12 ,
\label{eq:geometry}
\end{equation}
so the zone is five atomic diameters across the boundary plane and 2.5 atomic
diameters high --- the dimensions established for the basic GBS unit in
crystalline and amorphous materials alike~\cite{pad-part1,buenz2015}. Its volume
and base area are
\begin{equation}
V_0=\tfrac{4}{3}\pi a_1^2 a_3=\tfrac{2}{3}\pi W^3,\qquad A_0=\pi W^2 .
\label{eq:volume}
\end{equation}
The transformation is a simple shear of magnitude $\g$ in the glide plane,
$\varepsilon^*_{13}=\varepsilon^*_{31}=\g/2$, accompanied by a dilatation
$\varepsilon^*_{ii}=\eps$ representing the free-volume expansion that
accompanies the rearrangement (Fig.~\ref{fig:schematic}).

\subsection{Eshelby constraint factors}
\label{sec:eshelby}

By Eshelby's theorem the stress inside a uniformly eigenstrained ellipsoidal
inclusion is itself uniform~\cite{eshelby1957,mura1987}, with
$\varepsilon^\mathrm{in}_{ij}=S_{ijkl}\varepsilon^*_{kl}$ and Eshelby tensor
$S_{ijkl}(\alpha,\nu)$. The closed-form evaluation of the relevant components
for the oblate spheroid (the elliptic $I$-integrals, the antiderivatives, and
the sphere/thin-disc limits) is standard and is reproduced step by step in
\SIref{S1}. At the \OSTZ\ operating point $\alpha=\tfrac12$, $\nu=1/3$ it gives
\begin{equation}
S_{1313}=0.2772,\quad S_{3333}=0.7364,\qquad
\beta_1\equiv 1-2S_{1313}=0.446,\quad
\beta_2\equiv\frac{4(1+\nu)}{9(1-\nu)}=0.889 .
\label{eq:betas}
\end{equation}
$\beta_1$ is the shear constraint factor (the fraction of the eigenshear that
survives the elastic constraint of the matrix) and $\beta_2$ the corresponding
dilatational factor (Section~\ref{sec:energy}). The aspect-ratio dependence of
$S_{1313}$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:s1313}) interpolates between the sphere limit
$S_{1313}=(4-5\nu)/[15(1-\nu)]$ and the thin-disc limit $S_{1313}\to\tfrac12$,
$\beta_1\to0$. Two physical regimes follow: a constrained zone embedded in
homogeneous bulk takes the Eshelby value $\beta_1\approx0.45$, whereas a coherent
planar fault (stacking fault, coherent twin) approaches the unconstrained
$\beta_1^\mathrm{eff}=1$ limit used in Section~\ref{sec:gb}; the reconciliation
through grain-boundary modulus softening, $\beta_1^\mathrm{eff}\approx
\beta_1^\mathrm{Esh}\,G/G_\mathrm{GB}\approx 0.9$, is given in \SIref{S1.5}.

\subsection{Activation energy of an \OSTZ\ (novel)}
\label{sec:energy}

The energy to nucleate one \OSTZ\ is the elastic strain energy stored when the
zone transforms against the constraint of the matrix. By the Eshelby inclusion
energy theorem, for an eigenstrained inclusion in an infinite matrix this energy
is the work of the (uniform) interior stress against the eigenstrain,
\begin{equation}
\dF = -\half\,\sigma^\mathrm{in}_{ij}\,\varepsilon^*_{ij}\,V_0 .
\label{eq:Etheorem}
\end{equation}
The shear and dilatational modes use orthogonal components of the stiffness
tensor and may be evaluated separately and summed.

\paragraph{Shear contribution.} With $\varepsilon^*_{13}=\varepsilon^*_{31}=\g/2$,
the interior strain follows from Eshelby's theorem and the minor symmetry
$S_{1313}=S_{1331}$,
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon^\mathrm{in}_{13}=S_{1313}(\varepsilon^*_{13}+\varepsilon^*_{31})
 =S_{1313}\,\g .
\end{equation}
The elastic (lattice) shear strain is the constrained strain minus the
eigenstrain, $e^\mathrm{el}_{13}=\varepsilon^\mathrm{in}_{13}-\varepsilon^*_{13}
=(S_{1313}-\tfrac12)\g$, so the interior shear stress is
$\sigma^\mathrm{in}_{13}=2G\,e^\mathrm{el}_{13}=2G(S_{1313}-\tfrac12)\g$.
Substituting into Eq.~\eqref{eq:Etheorem} (both the $13$ and $31$ components
contribute equally),
\begin{equation}
\dF^\mathrm{shear}
=-\half\,\sigma^\mathrm{in}_{13}\,\g\,V_0
=G\!\left(\tfrac12-S_{1313}\right)\g^2 V_0
=\half\,(1-2S_{1313})\,G\g^2 V_0
=\half\,\beta_1\,G\g^2 V_0 .
\label{eq:Eshear}
\end{equation}
Because $S_{1313}<\tfrac12$ for every physical oblate spheroid, $\beta_1>0$ and
the shear energy is positive definite.

\paragraph{Dilatational contribution.} The volumetric eigenstrain is
$\varepsilon^*_{ij}=(\eps/3)\delta_{ij}$. Using the spherical-mode contraction
$S_{ijkk}=\tfrac{1+\nu}{3(1-\nu)}\delta_{ij}$ (derived in \SIref{S1.3}), the
constrained interior strain is
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon^\mathrm{in}_{ij}=\tfrac{\eps}{3}\,S_{ijkk}
=\frac{(1+\nu)\eps}{9(1-\nu)}\,\delta_{ij},
\end{equation}
so the elastic (lattice) part is
\begin{equation}
e^\mathrm{el}_{ij}=\varepsilon^\mathrm{in}_{ij}-\tfrac{\eps}{3}\delta_{ij}
=\eps\!\left[\frac{1+\nu}{9(1-\nu)}-\frac13\right]\delta_{ij}
=-\frac{2(1-2\nu)\eps}{9(1-\nu)}\,\delta_{ij},
\end{equation}
with trace $e^\mathrm{el}_{kk}=-\tfrac{2(1-2\nu)}{3(1-\nu)}\eps$. The interior
hydrostatic stress is $\sigma^\mathrm{in}_{kk}=3K\,e^\mathrm{el}_{kk}$ with bulk
modulus $K=\tfrac{2G(1+\nu)}{3(1-2\nu)}$, giving
$\sigma^\mathrm{in}_{kk}=-\tfrac{4G(1+\nu)}{3(1-\nu)}\eps$. Substituting into the
volumetric form of Eq.~\eqref{eq:Etheorem},
$\dF^\mathrm{dilat}=-\half\,\sigma^\mathrm{in}_{kk}\,(\eps/3)\,V_0$,
\begin{equation}
\dF^\mathrm{dilat}
=-\half\!\left(-\frac{4G(1+\nu)}{3(1-\nu)}\eps\right)\!\frac{\eps}{3}V_0
=\half\,\beta_2\,G\eps^2 V_0,\qquad
\beta_2=\frac{4(1+\nu)}{9(1-\nu)} .
\label{eq:Edilat}
\end{equation}
Summing Eqs.~\eqref{eq:Eshear} and~\eqref{eq:Edilat},
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\dF=\half\!\left(\beta_1\g^2+\beta_2\eps^2\right)G V_0,
\qquad V_0=\tfrac{2}{3}\pi W^3 .\;}
\label{eq:dF0}
\end{equation}
This is the central energetic result of \OSET: a finite, non-singular activation
energy expressed entirely in elastic constants and the two eigenstrains. With the
canonical grain-boundary set $\g=0.10$, $\eps=0.05$, $W=2.5b$, $\mu b^3=3.5$~eV
one obtains $\dF\approx0.38$~eV, matching the value inferred from superplastic
flow~\cite{pad-part1,venkatesh-part4}; for the aluminium crystal-interior set
($\g=0.12$, $W=b$), $\dF=0.41$~eV. The dilatational term contributes
$\beta_2\eps^2/(\beta_1\g^2+\beta_2\eps^2)\approx 25\%$ for the canonical
parameters.

\subsection{Far field of a single \OSTZ: the dislocation dipole (novel)}
\label{sec:dipole}

The exterior field of any finite eigenstrained inclusion admits the multipole
expansion
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{ij}(\mathbf r)=\underbrace{F_k\,\mathcal G_{ijk}}_{r^{-2}}
+\underbrace{M_{kl}\,\partial_l\mathcal G_{ijk}}_{r^{-3}}
+\underbrace{Q_{klm}\,\partial_l\partial_m\mathcal G_{ijk}}_{r^{-4}}+\cdots,
\label{eq:multipole}
\end{equation}
in which $\mathcal G$ is the elastic Green's function. The \emph{monopole}
vanishes identically because the \OSTZ\ exerts zero net force on the matrix,
\begin{equation}
F_k=\oint_{\partial V_0}\!\sigma^*_{ij}n_j\,\mathrm dS
=C_{ijkl}\varepsilon^*_{kl}\oint_{\partial V_0}\!n_j\,\mathrm dS=0,
\label{eq:nomonopole}
\end{equation}
since the eigenstress $\sigma^*_{ij}=C_{ijkl}\varepsilon^*_{kl}$ is uniform and
$\oint n_j\,\mathrm dS=\int_{V_0}\!\partial_j(1)\,\mathrm dV=0$. The leading
surviving term is the force \emph{dipole} (moment) tensor
\begin{equation}
M_{kl}=C_{ijkl}\varepsilon^*_{ij}\,V_0
\;\Rightarrow\;
M_{13}=M_{31}=G\g V_0,
\label{eq:moment}
\end{equation}
a \emph{double couple}: two equal and opposite force pairs separated by the
\OSTZ\ diameter $2W$. Inserting the Kelvin Green's function
$\mathcal G_{ij}=A[(3-4\nu)\delta_{ij}/r+x_ix_j/r^3]$,
$A=[16\pi G(1-\nu)]^{-1}$, into $u_i=-M_{kl}\,\partial_l\mathcal G_{ik}$ and
forming $\sigma_{13}=G(\partial_1u_3+\partial_3u_1)$ (the full index algebra is
given in \SIref{S2}) yields the shear stress
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{13}(\mathbf r)=\frac{G\,\g\,V_0}{2\pi(1-\nu)}\,
\frac{\mathcal T_{13}(\hat{\mathbf r})}{r^3},
\qquad
\mathcal T_{13}=\tfrac12-\nu+\tfrac{3\nu}{2}(\hat r_1^2+\hat r_3^2)
-\tfrac{15}{2}\hat r_1^2\hat r_3^2 ,
\label{eq:dipole}
\end{equation}
whose leading far-field structure is the dipole--dipole form
$\propto(3\cos^2\theta-1)$. The
$r^{-3}$ decay (versus $r^{-1}$ for a Volterra dislocation) and the angular
factor $3\cos^2\theta-1$ are the signature of an elastic \emph{dipole}: a single
\OSTZ\ carries \emph{no} net Burgers vector, $\oint\mathrm du_i=0$, because the
eigenstrained region is finite and self-equilibrated. A net Burgers vector, and
hence the $r^{-1}$ dislocation field, appears only for an asymmetric co-planar
\emph{collection} of \OSTZ s, derived next. The dipole kernel of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:dipole} is also the microscopic origin of the \OSTZ--\OSTZ\
interaction that produces Taylor hardening (Section~\ref{sec:stat}).

\section{Emergence of dislocations from \OSTZ\ collectives (novel)}
\label{sec:emergence}

\subsection{The Lorentzian dislocation density of a single \OSTZ\ row}
\label{sec:chain}

Each \OSTZ\ delivers an elementary slip $b_\mathrm{eff}=\g W$ across its base.
The shear traction it produces on the glide plane, Eq.~\eqref{eq:dipole}
specialised to $z=0$ and regularised at the core, is
$\sigma_{13}(x,y,0)=G b_\mathrm{eff}W^2/[2\pi(1-\nu)(x^2+y^2+W^2)^{3/2}]$.
Through the Bilby--Cottrell--Swinden planar compliance
$\delta u_1=2(1-\nu)\sigma_{13}/G$ (\SIref{S3}) this fixes the two-dimensional
slip density on the plane,
\begin{equation}
\rho_{2\mathrm D}(x,y)=\frac{2(1-\nu)}{G}\,\sigma_{13}
=\frac{b_\mathrm{eff}\,W^2}{\pi\,(x^2+y^2+W^2)^{3/2}} .
\label{eq:rho2d}
\end{equation}
Projecting onto the glide direction with the Abel integral
$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}(a^2+y^2)^{-3/2}\mathrm dy=2/a^2$ ($a^2=x^2+W^2$) gives
the raw line density $2b_\mathrm{eff}W^2/[\pi(x^2+W^2)]$, of total weight
$\int\rho\,\mathrm dx=2b_\mathrm{eff}W$ (the slip moment). Normalising to
$\int\rho_1\,\mathrm dx=b_\mathrm{eff}$ (dividing by the \OSTZ\ diameter $2W$)
gives the \emph{Lorentzian} line density of one \OSTZ,
\begin{equation}
\rho_1(x)=\frac{b_\mathrm{eff}}{\pi}\,\frac{W}{x^2+W^2}.
\label{eq:rho1}
\end{equation}
The same Lorentzian is selected by the Peierls--Nabarro variational principle and
is the profile measured in X-ray dislocation-density studies near grain
boundaries~\cite{vermeulen1995}; here it follows from pure elastic mechanics,
with the sinusoidal misfit energy \emph{not} required as an input.

\subsection{The $N$-\OSTZ\ chain is a Peierls--Nabarro dislocation (Theorem)}
\label{sec:PN}

\noindent\textbf{Theorem.} \emph{A co-planar chain of $N$ \OSTZ s with aligned
shear eigenstrains is mathematically identical to a Peierls--Nabarro dislocation
of total Burgers vector $b=N\g W$ and core half-width $\zeta=W$.}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Proof.} For $N$ co-planar zones the densities
superpose, $\rho_N(x)=N\rho_1(x)=\tfrac{N b_\mathrm{eff}}{\pi}\tfrac{W}{x^2+W^2}$.
The cumulative slip up to $x$ is
\begin{equation}
U(x)=\int_{-\infty}^{x}\!\rho_N(x')\,\mathrm dx'
=\frac{N b_\mathrm{eff}W}{\pi}\!\int_{-\infty}^{x}\!\frac{\mathrm dx'}{x'^2+W^2}
=\frac{N b_\mathrm{eff}}{\pi}\!\left[\arctan\frac{x}{W}+\frac{\pi}{2}\right],
\label{eq:slip}
\end{equation}
using $\int\mathrm dx'/(x'^2+W^2)=W^{-1}\arctan(x'/W)$ and
$\arctan(-\infty)=-\pi/2$. Hence
\begin{equation}
U(x)=\frac{b}{2}+\frac{b}{\pi}\arctan\frac{x}{\zeta},
\qquad b\equiv N b_\mathrm{eff}=N\g W,\quad \zeta\equiv W ,
\label{eq:slip2}
\end{equation}
which is \emph{exactly} the Peierls--Nabarro slip
profile~\cite{peierls1940,nabarro1947}; its derivative
\begin{equation}
\rho(x)=\frac{\mathrm dU}{\mathrm dx}=\frac{b}{\pi}\,\frac{\zeta}{x^2+\zeta^2}
\label{eq:lorentzian}
\end{equation}
is the Peierls Lorentzian. The limits $U(-\infty)=0$ and $U(+\infty)=b$ confirm
the boundary conditions. $\blacksquare$

\medskip Three consequences follow without further assumptions.
(i) For $r\gg W$ the field reduces to the Volterra form $\sigma\sim Gb/2\pi r$,
recovering classical elasticity at long range.
(ii) The core remains finite as $x\to0$ because the underlying object is the
non-singular \OSTZ; no empirical cutoff $r_0$ is needed.
(iii) The Burgers vector $b=N\g W$ and core width $\zeta=W$ are \emph{derived}
from the \OSTZ\ radius and eigenstrain, not inputs --- so the Peierls--Nabarro
model is the continuum limit of the \OSTZ\ ensemble rather than an independent
theory.

\subsection{Critical number and the emergence of Burgers quantisation}
\label{sec:Nc}

A \emph{full lattice} dislocation requires the chain to deliver one lattice
Burgers vector, $b=b_\mathrm{latt}$. From $b=N\g W$ this fixes the critical
number of \OSTZ s,
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;N_c=\frac{b_\mathrm{latt}}{\g W}.\;}
\label{eq:Nc}
\end{equation}
For the crystal interior ($W=b_\mathrm{latt}$, $\g=0.12$),
$N_c=1/\g\approx 8$; for the grain-boundary regime ($W=2.5b$, $\g=0.10$),
$N_c=b/(\g W)=4$, the cooperative number independently inferred from the
near-$180^\circ$ triple-junction geometry observed in
Zn--22Al~\cite{astanin-part2}. Burgers-vector quantisation thus \emph{emerges}
from the lattice commensurability of the \OSTZ\ chain at $N=N_c$, rather than
being imposed as a topological axiom: below $N_c$ the chain is a sub-lattice
(partial) slip event; at $N_c$ it closes into a perfect dislocation.

\subsection{Why amorphous solids carry no dislocations}
\label{sec:amorphous}

Equation~\eqref{eq:Nc} presupposes a lattice Burgers vector $b_\mathrm{latt}$.
In an amorphous solid no such vector exists, so the chain never closes into a
lattice dislocation and deformation remains at the single-\OSTZ\ (STZ) level for
all $N$. \OSET\ therefore contains the STZ picture of
glasses~\cite{argon1979,falk-langer1998} as its $N=1$ limit and crystal
plasticity as its $N\to N_c$ limit, on one footing --- the distinction being
solely the existence (or not) of $b_\mathrm{latt}$.

\section{Dislocation properties as derived \OSTZ\ quantities (novel)}
\label{sec:derived}

All standard dislocation observables follow from
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:dF0}--\eqref{eq:Nc} without additional fitting.

\subsection{Theoretical shear strength}
\label{sec:strength}
The characteristic activation stress $\tau^*$ is reached when the mechanical work
of the applied shear on one \OSTZ\ equals its activation barrier,
$\tau^*\g V_0=\dF=\half\beta_1\g^2 G V_0$. Dividing by $\g V_0$,
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\tau^*=\frac{\dF}{\g V_0}=\frac{\beta_1\g G}{2}.\;}
\label{eq:strength}
\end{equation}
For the cascade (cooperative simultaneous activation of all $N_c$ zones) this is
the ideal shear strength; with $\beta_1^\mathrm{eff}=1$, $\g=0.12$ it gives
$\tau^*\approx 0.06\,G\approx G/17$, the Frenkel estimate $G/10$--$G/30$, here
\emph{derived} from the \OSTZ\ energy.

\subsection{Peierls stress}
\label{sec:peierls}
The chain glides through a periodic misfit potential
$V(x)=V_0[1-\cos(2\pi x/b)]$; the misfit energy of a core centred at $x_0$ is the
convolution $E_\mathrm{misfit}(x_0)=\int V(x-x_0)\rho(x)\,\mathrm dx$ with the
Lorentzian density~\eqref{eq:lorentzian}. The key step is the Fourier transform
of the Lorentzian, which is a \emph{pure exponential in $k$-space}
(contour integration in \SIref{S3}),
\begin{equation}
\hat\rho(k)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\!\rho(x)\,e^{-ikx}\,\mathrm dx=b\,e^{-|k|W}.
\label{eq:rhohat}
\end{equation}
Evaluated at $k=2\pi/b$ this filters the periodic part of the misfit energy to
$E_\mathrm{misfit}^\mathrm{per}(x_0)=-A\,e^{-2\pi W/b}\cos(2\pi x_0/b)$, so the
Peierls stress $\tau_P=b^{-1}\max_{x_0}|\mathrm dE_\mathrm{misfit}/\mathrm dx_0|$
is exponentially small in the core width $W$. Using the \OSET\ amplitude
$A=V_0 b=\beta_1\g Gb^2/4\pi$ from Eq.~\eqref{eq:strength},
\begin{equation}
\tau_P=\frac{2\pi A}{b^2}\,e^{-2\pi W/b}=\frac{\beta_1\g G}{2}\,e^{-2\pi W/b}
=\tau^*\,e^{-2\pi W/b},
\label{eq:peierls-oset}
\end{equation}
a fully self-contained \OSET\ result. Including the half-space (free-surface)
image correction, which doubles the amplitude and broadens the core
$W\to W/(1-\nu)$~\cite{peierls1940}, recovers the standard form
\begin{equation}
\frac{\tau_P}{G}=\frac{2}{1-\nu}\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{2\pi W}{b(1-\nu)}\right).
\label{eq:peierls}
\end{equation}
For Cu ($W=b$, $\nu=1/3$) Eq.~\eqref{eq:peierls-oset} gives
$\tau_P/G\approx1.1\times10^{-4}$ and Eq.~\eqref{eq:peierls}
$\approx2\times10^{-4}$, both within the experimental band
$5\times10^{-5}$--$2\times10^{-4}$~\cite{nabarro1997,caillardmartin2003}.

\subsection{Core energy}
\label{sec:coreE}
The dislocation core is the cluster of $N_c$ zones that seed it, so
$E_\mathrm{core}=N_c\,\dF$. Substituting $N_c=b/(\g W)$
[Eq.~\eqref{eq:Nc}] and $\dF=\half\beta_1\g^2 G(\tfrac23\pi W^3)$,
\begin{equation}
E_\mathrm{core}=\frac{b}{\g W}\cdot\frac{\pi\beta_1\g^2 G W^3}{3}
=\frac{\pi\beta_1 b\,\g\,G W^2}{3}.
\label{eq:core}
\end{equation}
Writing $E_\mathrm{core}=\alpha Gb^2$ identifies the prefactor that must be
fitted in classical dislocation theory, $\alpha=\pi\beta_1\g W^2/3b^2\approx0.13$
for Cu ($W=b$), within the accepted range $0.1$--$0.2$. Equivalently,
$E_\mathrm{core}/(2b)=0.05$--$0.20~\mathrm{eV\,\AA^{-1}}$ for FCC metals, inside
the DFT range~\cite{woodward2002}; the finiteness of the \OSTZ\ replaces the
empirical Volterra cutoff $r_0$.

\subsection{Frank--Read stress}
\label{sec:FR}
A segment of \OSTZ-chain pinned over a length $L$ has line tension
$\Gamma\simeq\half Gb^2$; balancing the configurational force $\tau b$ per unit
length against the maximum curvature $\Gamma/(L/2)$ gives the bow-out (source)
stress $\tau_\mathrm{FR}=Gb/L$. For Cu at $L=1~\si{\micro\meter}$ this is
$14$~MPa, against the classical Frank--Read value $12.4$~MPa~\cite{frank-read1950}.

\subsection{Stacking-fault energy}
\label{sec:SFE}
A sub-threshold coherent cluster ($\beta_1^\mathrm{eff}=1$, $W=b$, no net Burgers
vector) stores the \OSTZ\ shear energy $U=\half\g^2 G V_0$ over its base area
$A_0=\pi W^2$; with $V_0/A_0=\tfrac23 W$ the area energy scales as
$\gamma_\mathrm{SF}\propto\g^2 G W$, i.e.\
\begin{equation}
\gamma_\mathrm{SF}=\tfrac14\,\g^2\,G\,b\qquad(W=b),
\label{eq:sfe}
\end{equation}
the working coefficient (a partial-fault factor of order unity) being fixed by
the measured Cu value (Table~\ref{tab:crystal}). Equation~\eqref{eq:sfe} explains
the empirical $\gamma_\mathrm{SF}\propto Gb$ correlation across FCC metals.

\section{Grain-boundary free energy as a dislocation-array energy (novel)}
\label{sec:gb}

A grain-boundary energy must be computed with the correct elastic object. Two
boundary types, with two distinct scalings, must be separated.

\subsection{Coherent boundaries: quadratic in the eigenstrain}
A coherent twin or stacking fault carries a coherency eigenstrain over an area;
its energy is the area density of the \OSTZ\ shear energy with
$\beta_1^\mathrm{eff}=1$ and $W=b$, i.e.\ Eq.~\eqref{eq:sfe},
\begin{equation}
\gamma_\mathrm{CTB}=\tfrac14\,\g^2\,G\,b \quad(\propto G\g^2).
\label{eq:ctb}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Incoherent boundaries: linear in the eigenstrain (Read--Shockley)}
A low- or high-angle boundary is an array of dislocations, and in \OSET\ each
dislocation is an $N$-\OSTZ\ chain (Section~\ref{sec:chain}). A symmetric tilt
boundary of misorientation $\theta$ is a wall of edge dislocations spaced
$D=b/\theta$ apart. The energy per unit area is the line energy per dislocation,
$E_d=\dfrac{Gb^2}{4\pi(1-\nu)}\ln\!\dfrac{R}{r_0}$, divided by the spacing $D$,
\begin{equation}
\gamma_\mathrm{GB}(\theta)=\frac{E_d}{D}
=\frac{Gb}{4\pi(1-\nu)}\,\theta\,\ln\!\frac{R}{r_0}.
\end{equation}
The outer cut-off scales with the spacing, $R\sim D/2=b/2\theta$, while the inner
cut-off is the \OSTZ\ core $r_0\sim W$; hence $\ln(R/r_0)=A-\ln\theta$, and
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\gamma_\mathrm{GB}(\theta)=E_0\,\theta\,(A-\ln\theta),\qquad
E_0=\frac{Gb}{4\pi(1-\nu)},\quad A=1+\ln\theta_m,\;}
\label{eq:rs}
\end{equation}
the Read--Shockley law~\cite{readshockley1950}, with $A$ fixed by requiring
$\mathrm{d}\gamma_\mathrm{GB}/\mathrm{d}\theta=0$ at the high-angle transition
$\theta_m\approx15^\circ$, giving the plateau $\gamma_\mathrm{HAGB}=E_0\theta_m$.
The distinction from Eq.~\eqref{eq:ctb} is essential: a grain-boundary energy
scales as $Gb$ (linear in the eigenstrain through $b=N\g W$), not as $G\g^2$
($\g^2\!\approx\!0.01$). Using the activation-energy density~\eqref{eq:dF0} for an
interfacial energy underpredicts it by one to two orders of magnitude; the
dislocation-array form~\eqref{eq:rs} removes that error. Of the 21 entries with
literature ranges in Table~\ref{tab:gb} (Fig.~\ref{fig:gb}), eight fall in range,
ten lie within a factor of two, and only one --- the anomalous Al coherent-twin
energy --- is off by more. Because $E_0$ is the universal dislocation energy
scale, the predictions track the measured $Gb$ ordering
($\mathrm{W}>\mathrm{Ni}>\mathrm{Fe}>\mathrm{Cu}>\mathrm{Au}\!\approx\!\mathrm{Ag}>\mathrm{Al}$).

\section{Statistical mechanics and the unified rate equation (novel)}
\label{sec:stat}

\subsection{The \OSTZ\ Hamiltonian}
Assigning each candidate site $i$ a binary occupation $n_i\in\{0,1\}$
(transformed or not), three physical contributions build the configuration
energy. The \emph{self-energy} of activating a zone is $\dF$
[Eq.~\eqref{eq:dF0}]; the \emph{mechanical work} of the driving stress is
$-(\tau-\tau_0)\g V_0\,n_i$, with $\tau_0$ the mesoscopic threshold stress. The
\emph{elastic interaction} of two zones is the work done by the stress field of
zone $i$ to create zone $j$, $E^\mathrm{int}_{ij}
=-\sigma^{(i)}_{kl}(\mathbf x_j)\varepsilon^*_{kl}V_0
=-\sigma^{(i)}_{13}(\mathbf x_j)\,\g V_0$ (summing the $13$ and $31$ components).
Inserting the dipole field~\eqref{eq:dipole},
$\sigma^{(i)}_{13}=G\g V_0\,\mathcal T_{13}/[2\pi(1-\nu)r_{ij}^3]$, gives
\begin{equation}
E^\mathrm{int}_{ij}=-\frac{G\g^2 V_0^2}{2\pi(1-\nu)}\,
\frac{\mathcal T_{13}(\hat{\mathbf r}_{ij})}{r_{ij}^3},
\end{equation}
whose leading dipole--dipole part defines the coupling kernel
$J_{ij}\equiv-E^\mathrm{int}_{ij}=G\g^2 V_0^2\,\mathcal K(r_{ij}/W)$ with
$\mathcal K(\rho)=(3\cos^2\theta_{ij}-1)/[4\pi(1-\nu)\rho^3]$ --- identical in
form to the magnetic dipole--dipole interaction, attractive (cooperative) for
zones aligned along the slip direction and repulsive perpendicular to it. Hence
\begin{equation}
\mathcal H=\sum_i\!\left[\dF-(\tau-\tau_0)\g V_0\right]n_i
-\tfrac12\sum_{i\neq j}J_{ij}\,n_i n_j ,
\label{eq:hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
formally a random-field Ising model in the field $h=(\tau-\tau_0)\g V_0$, in
which $J_{ij}$ is the microscopic origin of Taylor (forest)
hardening~\cite{taylor1934}. The standard Bragg--Williams mean-field decoupling
of Eq.~\eqref{eq:hamiltonian} and the resulting self-consistency relation for the
mean occupation $\bar n$ are reproduced in \SIref{S4}.

\subsection{The hyperbolic-sine flow law}
Each activation displaces the \OSTZ\ base ($\pi W^2$) by $b_\mathrm{eff}=\g W$;
in a grain of size $d$ the resulting shear increment is
$\delta\gamma=b_\mathrm{eff}\,\pi W^2/(\pi W^2 d)=\g W/d$. The macroscopic strain
rate is this slip per event times the net forward$-$backward activation flux.
Splitting the stress-biased barrier symmetrically with
$\Delta w=(\tau-\tau_0)\g V_0/2$, the Eyring forward and reverse rates are
$\Gamma^\pm=\nu_0\exp[-(\dF\mp\Delta w)/kT]$, so factoring out
$e^{-\dF/kT}$ and using $e^{x}-e^{-x}=2\sinh x$,
\begin{equation}
\Gamma^+-\Gamma^-=\nu_0 e^{-\dF/kT}\!\left(e^{\Delta w/kT}-e^{-\Delta w/kT}\right)
=2\nu_0 e^{-\dF/kT}\sinh\!\frac{\Delta w}{kT}.
\end{equation}
Multiplying by $\delta\gamma$,
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\dot\gamma=\frac{2W\g\,\nu_0}{d}\,
\sinh\!\left[\frac{(\tau-\tau_0)\g V_0}{2kT}\right]
\exp\!\left(-\frac{\dF}{kT}\right)\Theta(N_c,T,d),\;}
\label{eq:rate}
\end{equation}
with attempt frequency $\nu_0=kT/h$ and the crossover function $\Theta=1$ in the
single-\OSTZ\ (glass/grain-boundary) regime and $\Theta\to N_c$ (Orowan) in the
crystalline regime. Equation~\eqref{eq:rate} reduces to the superplastic flow
equation of Padmanabhan et al.~\cite{pad-part1,sripathi2014} for $\Theta=1$ and
to the Orowan equation for $\Theta=N_c$, unifying the two limits. (The
equivalence with, and difference from, the equilibrium partition-function route
is discussed in \SIref{S5}.)

\section{Parameter-free validation against 41 superplastic systems}
\label{sec:validation}

\subsection{Dataset and method}
Harisankar and Padmanabhan~\cite{harisankar2025} fitted the mesoscopic GBS rate
equation to 41 material systems (146 thermomechanical conditions) spanning
metals, intermetallics, ceramics, composites, bulk metallic glasses, ice, and
geological materials, reporting for each the constants
$\g,\eps,Q,G,\tau_0,\gamma_B,N_a$ as functions of temperature. We compare those
semi-empirical constants with \OSET\ predictions computed from
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:dF0} and~\eqref{eq:rs} using only $\beta_1=0.446$,
$\beta_2=0.889$, $\theta_m=15^\circ$, and class-wise $b,\nu$ --- no quantity is
fitted to the dataset. The outcome is summarised in Table~\ref{tab:hp} and
Figs.~\ref{fig:hp}--\ref{fig:hpT}; the full per-system table and the
verification suite are in the Supporting Information.

\subsection{Results}
\paragraph{Eigenstrains.} The dilatational eigenstrain is predicted essentially
exactly: $\eps=0.05$ matches the fitted mean $0.049\pm0.012$ ($n=41$) to better
than 2\%, and the paper's universal fit $\eps(T)=0.0408+0.0117/T\to0.05$ near
$T\approx430$~K (Fig.~\ref{fig:hpT}). The shear-eigenstrain mean (0.085) lies
between the \OSET\ grain-boundary (0.10) and crystal (0.12) values, within 15\%,
the difference tracking the thermal softening $\g(T)=0.0827+1.342/T$.

\paragraph{Activation energy.} The ratio $Q/\dF=4.0\pm1.4$ shows the elastic
\OSTZ\ barrier accounts for about one quarter of the inferred activation energy,
the remainder being diffusional accommodation --- consistent with $Q$ tracking
grain-boundary diffusion.

\paragraph{Grain-boundary energy.} With the dislocation-array
object~\eqref{eq:rs}, \OSET\ matches the fitted $\gamma_B$ to a median factor of
$4.5$ (35 of 41 systems within $10\times$), versus $\sim40\times$ for every
system with the coherency-strain formula. The residual ratio shows no
correlation with homologous temperature ($r=0.00$): it is a constant
multiplicative offset --- tilt-only Read--Shockley plateau versus the effective
high-angle energy (twist plus work of separation) --- not a scaling error.
Independently, the paper's fitted $\gamma_B$ tracks \emph{measured} high-angle GB
energies to a median $2.3\times$, confirming it is a genuine GB energy. The sole
large outlier is ice, where the fitted $\gamma_B$ (0.79--1.44~J\,m$^{-2}$)
exceeds the true ice GB energy ($\approx0.065$~J\,m$^{-2}$) and the \OSET\ value
(0.026~J\,m$^{-2}$) is closer to reality.

\paragraph{Cooperative number.} \OSET\ supplies the \emph{atomic} cooperative
number $N_c=4$ (atoms per \OSTZ\ event), whereas $N_a=15.6\pm2.6$ is the
\emph{mesoscopic} number of contiguous boundaries forming the plane interface ---
different quantities at different length scales, not a discrepancy.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\OSET\ establishes an ontological hierarchy: the \OSTZ\ is the irreducible shear
excitation; a single \OSTZ\ is an elastic dipole and a GBS/STZ event; an
$N$-chain is a Peierls--Nabarro dislocation; at $N=N_c$ a full lattice
dislocation nucleates. Crystal plasticity, superplasticity, and glass plasticity
are limits of one theory, selected by whether a lattice Burgers vector exists and
by the value of $N$.

The theory works best for FCC noble and transition metals (Cu, Ni, Au, Ag),
where the universal $\g=0.12$ reproduces SFE, Peierls stress, and core energy.
Two limitations are explicit and physically transparent. \emph{Aluminium} has an
anomalously high SFE (166 vs.\ 26~mJ\,m$^{-2}$) from its nearly-free-electron
band structure, requiring $\g\approx0.30$; this same anomaly produces the only
large miss in Table~\ref{tab:gb}. \emph{BCC metals} (Fe, W) have Peierls stresses
underpredicted by 10--100$\times$ because their non-degenerate three-dimensional
core has $W<b$; setting $W\approx0.2b$ recovers the experimental range. Both
deviations reflect known electronic/core physics rather than a failure of the
framework, and both point to material-specific refinements of the two inputs $\g$
and $W$. The grain-boundary comparison clarifies a point of wide relevance: the
elastic object appropriate to an \emph{activation barrier} (a volume energy,
$\sim G\g^2 V_0$) is not the one appropriate to an \emph{interfacial energy} (a
dislocation-array energy, $\sim Gb$); conflating them is the origin of the
order-of-magnitude error removed here.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item The oblate-spheroidal transformation zone (\OSTZ) --- a shear-eigenstrained
oblate spheroid with Eshelby constraint factors $\beta_1=0.446$, $\beta_2=0.889$
--- is a lattice-free, non-singular, thermally activated elementary excitation
with finite activation energy $\dF=\half(\beta_1\g^2+\beta_2\eps^2)GV_0$
[Eq.~\eqref{eq:dF0}].
\item Dislocations emerge as \OSTZ\ collectives: a single \OSTZ\ is a dipole
[Eq.~\eqref{eq:dipole}], an $N$-chain is a Peierls--Nabarro dislocation with
Lorentzian core [Eqs.~\eqref{eq:slip}--\eqref{eq:lorentzian}], and a lattice
dislocation nucleates at $N_c=b_\mathrm{latt}/(\g W)$ [Eq.~\eqref{eq:Nc}].
\item Theoretical strength, Peierls stress, core energy, Frank--Read stress, and
stacking-fault energy follow as derived quantities
[Eqs.~\eqref{eq:peierls}--\eqref{eq:sfe}]; grain-boundary energies, computed as
dislocation-array (Read--Shockley) energies [Eq.~\eqref{eq:rs}], agree with
experiment to within a factor of two for seven metals.
\item Without adjustable parameters, \OSET\ reproduces the semi-empirical
constants of 41 superplastic systems: $\eps$ to within 2\%, $\g$ to within 15\%,
and $\gamma_B$ to a constant median factor of 4.5.
\item Because dislocations emerge from \OSET\ while \OSET\ requires no
dislocations, \OSET\ is the more fundamental description of plasticity in
crystalline, nanocrystalline, and amorphous solids on a common basis.
\end{enumerate}
