Item 1: ΔF0 equation form
-----------------------------------------------
OSET claim: ΔF0 = (1/2)(β1·γ0² + β2·ε0²)·G·V0   [Eq. 8]

MSEA paper (Eq. 1, page 7):
  "ΔF0 = (1/2)(β1·γ0² + β2·ε0²)·G·V0"
  (extracted as: "ΔF0 = 1/2(β1·γ0² + β2·ε0²)·G·V0 (1)")

MSEA Appendix (Eq. A.3, page 12):
  "ΔF0 = (1/2)(β1·γ0² + β2·ε0²)·G·V0;  ε0 = γ0/√3  (von Mises yielding is assumed)"

STATUS: MATCHES. The functional form of the activation energy equation is
  identical in OSET (Eq. 8) and both MSEA equations (Eq. 1 and A.3).
  The MSEA paper additionally specifies the von Mises relationship ε0 = γ0/√3,
  which is consistent with the KAP-Gleiter paper's equation (8): "3γ0 = C·(ΔV/V0),
  C = √3 for von Mises".

NOTE: The MSEA Appendix note says "ε0 = γ0/√3" while OSET's Table (§2.3) says
  G0 = ε0 = 0.05 for γ0 = 0.10. These differ slightly: γ0/√3 = 0.10/1.732 = 0.0577,
  not 0.05. Minor discrepancy (~13%).

Item 2: Values of γ0, W, β1, β2, G0
-----------------------------------------------
OSET canonical values (§2.3, for Al grain boundaries):
  W = 2.5b ≈ 0.75 nm;  γ0 = 0.10;  G0 = ε0 = 0.05;  β1 = 0.446;  β2 = 0.889

MSEA paper (page 7–8):
  W = "grain boundary width, W = 2.5 a0" (a0 = atomic diameter of major constituent)
  For Al: a0 ≈ b = 0.286 nm → W ≈ 0.715 nm.
  For Cu: a0 ≈ b = 0.256 nm → W ≈ 0.64 nm.
  V0 = (2/3)πW³  [confirmed on page 7: "volume of the spheroid = (2/3)πW³"]

  β1 formula (page 7):
    "For an oblate spheroid β1 = 0.944[(1.590 − p)/(1 − p)]  and
     β2 = [4(1+p)/9(1 − p)], with p the Poisson ratio [8]."
    [Reference [8] = Eshelby 1957]

  Computed β1 for ν = p = 1/3:
    β1 = 0.944 × (1.590 − 0.333)/(1 − 0.333) = 0.944 × 1.257/0.667 = 0.944 × 1.884 ≈ 1.779

  Computed β2 for ν = 1/3:
    β2 = 4(1 + 1/3)/[9(1 − 1/3)] = 4(4/3)/[9(2/3)] = (16/3)/6 = 8/9 ≈ 0.889

  γ0 values (Appendix Table A1, computed for Cu via iterative fitting):
    Row-by-row: 0.1443, 0.1454, 0.1465, 0.1472, 0.1387, 0.1398, 0.1420
    Mean γ0 ≈ 0.1434  (range 0.139–0.147)

  ε0 = γ0/√3 ≈ 0.0828 mean (Table A1 column confirmed: 0.0833, 0.0839, …, 0.0828 mean)

STATUS — W: MATCHES OSET's W = 2.5b (same formula, slightly different
  numerical result depending on whether a0 or b is used).

STATUS — β2: MATCHES OSET's spherical approximation β2 = 4(1+ν)/[9(1-ν)] = 0.889.

STATUS — β1: ✗ MAJOR DISCREPANCY.
  MSEA paper gives β1 = 0.944(1.590−ν)/(1−ν) ≈ 1.779 for ν=1/3.
  OSET claims β1 = 1 − 2S1313 = 0.4456 ("Eshelby value", §2.2).
  OSET also claims "Padmanabhan et al. framework uses β1^eff ≈ 1" (§2.2a).
  NEITHER 0.446 NOR 1.0 matches the paper's explicit formula of ≈1.78.
  The paper's β1 formula is approximately 4× larger than OSET's Eshelby value.
  Origin of discrepancy: The papers cite Eshelby (1957) [Ref. 8] for β1, but OSET
  derives β1 = 1 − 2S1313 directly from the Eshelby inclusion theorem with
  eigenstrain ε*13 = γ0/2. The formula 0.944(1.590−ν)/(1−ν) does not appear
  derivable from S1313 for α=0.5, and must come from either a different reference
  or a different approximation (possibly Argon 1979 or an earlier calculation).

STATUS — γ0: PARTIAL MATCH / DISCREPANCY.
  KAP-Gleiter paper (2012) says γ0 ≈ 0.10 (from Argon/bubble raft experiments).
  MSEA paper (2024) COMPUTES γ0 iteratively from Cu GB data: mean γ0 ≈ 0.143
  (range 0.139–0.147). This is significantly higher than OSET's "canonical γ0 = 0.10".
  The value 0.10 appears to be a legacy assumption that the newer MSEA paper revises.

STATUS — G0 = ε0: PARTIAL MATCH.
  OSET: G0 = 0.05 for γ0 = 0.10 (= γ0/2).
  Papers: ε0 = γ0/√3 (von Mises), which for γ0 = 0.10 gives 0.0577, and for the
  computed mean γ0 = 0.143 gives 0.0828. OSET's value of 0.05 corresponds to
  γ0 = 0.087 using von Mises, or to γ0/2 = 0.05 using a non-standard convention.
  Minor to moderate discrepancy depending on choice of γ0.

Item 4: N_c value
-----------------------------------------------
OSET claim: N_c = 4 (critical OSTZ number for GB sliding; from Eq. 19a:
  N_c = b/(γ0·2.5b) = 1/(0.1×2.5) = 4).

MSEA paper: Uses "N" = number of grain boundaries that align to form the
  mesoscopic plane interface (threshold stress formula Eq. 3 / Appendix Eq. A.4):

STATUS: ✗ THESE ARE DIFFERENT QUANTITIES. OSET's N_c = 4 is the number of
  OSTZs that must cooperate to produce one lattice Burgers vector in the GB regime
  (a microscopic / OSTZ-chain quantity derived in §3.3). The MSEA paper's N is
  the mesoscopic number of grain boundaries forming a plane interface (computed
  from the threshold stress equation by fitting to experimental data). Mean N ≈ 7
  for Cu. OSET does not acknowledge this distinction. While OSET claims N_c = 4
  for the GB sliding regime, the MSEA paper computes N = 4–11 (mean ≈ 7) for Cu
  as the mesoscopic cooperativity — these are conceptually and numerically distinct.

Item 8: Strain rate sensitivity m and cooperativity
-----------------------------------------------
OSET claim: m = d(ln τ)/d(ln γ̇) depends on N_c; m→1 for individual OSTZ events
  (glass/GB); m decreases as N_c increases (crystalline).

MSEA paper (pages 9–10 and Section 3):
  Does NOT derive m directly from N_c. Instead uses the internal stress distribution
  approach. From the lognormal internal stress distribution with parameters τ_m, λ:
  "n (= 1/m) is given by: [Eq. 12 — complex expression involving τ_m, λ, τ0]"
  The paper states: "once τ_m and k are known, it is possible to determine the value
  of the stress exponent, and hence the strain-rate sensitivity index, for any given
  material in any material class."
  The paper references the cooperativity N in the context of the threshold stress
  (Eq. 3), not strain-rate sensitivity directly.

  For bulk metallic glasses / poly-glasses: "the rate equation gives b = 1 [i.e., m=1]
  and minimal grain deformation" — matches OSET's m→1 for glass/GB limit.

STATUS: PARTIAL MATCH. The qualitative trend (m→1 for glasses, m<1 for crystalline
  materials) is consistent. However, the quantitative link between m and OSET's N_c
  is not explicit in the MSEA paper — the paper uses the lognormal stress distribution
  to compute m, not the OSTZ cooperativity number.

   SOURCE: MSEA Section 7 p.10 "oblate spheroids containing free volume as

B. Claims that DIFFER in the papers (exact differences documented)
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

1. β1 VALUE — MAJOR DISCREPANCY

   OSET claims:
     (a) "Exact Eshelby value: β1 = 1 − 2S1313 = 0.4456 for ν=1/3, α=0.5" (§2.2)
     (b) "Padmanabhan et al. framework uses β1^eff ≈ 1" (§2.2a, due to GB softening)
   
   Papers actually state:
     MSEA p.7: "β1 = 0.944[(1.590 − p)/(1 − p)]"   → 1.779 for ν=1/3
     KAP-Gleiter Eq.(3): "β1 = 0.944(1.590−p)/(1−p)"   → 1.779 for ν=1/3
   
   Comparison: 0.4456 (OSET) ≠ 0.944(1.257/0.667) ≈ 1.78 (papers) ≠ 1.0 (OSET §2.2a)
   
   This is a factor of ~4 difference. The papers consistently use the formula
   0.944(1.590−ν)/(1−ν). Neither OSET's "Eshelby-derived 0.446" nor its
   "effective GB-softened 1.0" matches what the papers actually compute.
   
   NOTE: The papers cite Eshelby (1957) as the source of β1, but OSET's own
   derivation from the same Eshelby formalism gives 0.4456. The formula
   0.944(1.590−ν)/(1−ν) may originate from the 1977 Padmanabhan paper or from
   Gittus (1975), using a different approximation for the oblate spheroid shear
   constraint — this requires further investigation.

2. ΔF0 NUMERICAL VALUE

   OSET:  ΔF0 = 0.38 eV = 39.5 kJ/mol  (for Al GB, using β1=0.446, γ0=0.10,
          G_Al=22 GPa, W=2.5b≈0.75nm)
   
   MSEA paper (Table A1, for Cu, iteratively fitted):
     ΔF0 = 162–180 kJ/mol (mean 172.5 kJ/mol = 1.79 eV)
   
   MSEA paper (Section 6, MD-assisted, Cu Σ41 GB):
     ΔF0 = 65.2 kJ/mol (using Neier et al. γ0=0.10, G_GB=24 GPa)
   
   Discrepancy factors:
     OSET's 0.38 eV vs. MSEA computed 1.79 eV: factor ≈ 4.7×
     OSET's 0.38 eV vs. MSEA MD-based 65.2 kJ/mol: factor ≈ 1.7×
   
   The 4.7× discrepancy arises mainly from β1 (×4 difference) and the difference
   in γ0 and G values. OSET's value of 39.5 kJ/mol is significantly lower than
   the values the papers actually compute for any material.

3. γ0 VALUE (2024 MSEA paper vs. earlier papers)

   OSET canonical: γ0 = 0.10 (for GB regime)
   KAP-Gleiter (2012): γ0 ≈ 0.10 (assumed, consistent with OSET)
   MSEA (2024, Cu): γ0 = 0.139–0.147 (mean 0.143, computed iteratively from Eq. A.3)
   
   The MSEA 2024 paper computes γ0 ≈ 0.143 for Cu, which is ~43% higher than
   the canonical value of 0.10. The OSET "canonical" value is consistent with the
   older papers but not the 2024 computation.

4. N (MESOSCOPIC COOPERATIVITY) vs. N_c (OSTZ COOPERATIVITY)

   OSET: N_c = 4 (number of OSTZs to form one Burgers vector; §3.3, Eq. 19a)
   MSEA paper: N = mean ≈ 7.0 (number of grain boundaries forming plane interface;
     Table A1, range 3.9–11.2 depending on T and grain size)
   
   These are different physical quantities. OSET's N_c is a microscopic OSTZ-chain
   parameter; the papers' N is mesoscopic (related to grain-scale geometry).
   Direct comparison is not appropriate, but both indicate a cooperativity of
   order 4–11 at some relevant scale.

Based on the comparison, the following corrections and clarifications are recommended

-------------------------------------------------------------------
CORRECTION 1 (CRITICAL): β1 formula discrepancy
-------------------------------------------------------------------
CURRENT OSET TEXT (§2.2, Table §2.3):
  "β1 = 1 − 2S1313 = 0.4456 for ν=1/3"
  "Padmanabhan et al. framework uses β1^eff ≈ 1"

ACTUAL PAPERS (MSEA p.7; KAP-Gleiter Eq.3):
  β1 = 0.944(1.590 − ν)/(1 − ν) ≈ 1.779 for ν = 1/3

REQUIRED CORRECTION:
  Acknowledge explicitly that the published papers use the formula
  β1 = 0.944(1.590−ν)/(1−ν) ≈ 1.78, NOT the Eshelby-derived 0.446 or the
  GB-softened effective value of 1.0. The OSET claims of "canonical β1 = 0.446"
  and "papers use β1^eff ≈ 1" both misrepresent what the papers say.
  
  Action: Investigate the origin of the formula 0.944(1.590−ν)/(1−ν). It may come
  from Argon (1979), Gittus (1975), or the original 1977 Padmanabhan paper. Add
  a clarifying note distinguishing:
    (a) OSET Eshelby value: β1 = 1 − 2S1313 = 0.4456
    (b) Papers' formula: β1 = 0.944(1.590−ν)/(1−ν) ≈ 1.78
    (c) Where they come from and why they differ

CORRECTION 2 (MAJOR): ΔF0 "canonical" value
-------------------------------------------------------------------
CURRENT OSET TEXT (§2.3):
  "ΔF0 = 0.38 eV = 39.5 kJ/mol (canonical parameter values from Padmanabhan et al. 1996)"

ACTUAL PAPERS:
  KAP-Gleiter (2012): Uses β1 ≈ 1.78, γ0 ≈ 0.10 → computes ΔF0 per material
  MSEA (2024), Table A1: ΔF0(Cu) = 162–180 kJ/mol (mean 1.79 eV)

CORRECTION 3 (MODERATE): γ0 "canonical" value
-------------------------------------------------------------------
CURRENT OSET TEXT (§2.3):
  "γ0 = 0.10 (canonical, for Al grain boundaries)"

ACTUAL STATUS:
  KAP-Gleiter (2012): γ0 ≈ 0.10 (matches, based on Argon analogy)

CORRECTION 4 (MODERATE): N_c = 4 interpretation
-------------------------------------------------------------------
CURRENT OSET TEXT (§3.3, Table §2.3):
  "N_c = 4 (critical OSTZ number for GB sliding regime)"
  Derived as: N_c = b/(γ0·2.5b) = 1/(0.1×2.5) = 4

ACTUAL PAPERS:
  The papers use N for the number of grain boundaries forming a mesoscopic plane
  interface (MSEA Table A1: mean N ≈ 7 for Cu). KAP-Gleiter uses N1 for the number

  Neither paper derives or uses OSET's "N_c = 4 OSTZs per Burgers vector" concept.

RECOMMENDED CORRECTION:
  Clarify that "N_c = 4" is a new OSET derivation, NOT taken directly from the
  papers. State explicitly that the papers' "N" refers to a different quantity
  (mesoscopic plane-interface boundary count). The relationship between these
  two quantities should be discussed.

CORRECTION 7 (CLARIFICATION): Eshelby formula sourcing for β1
-------------------------------------------------------------------
The OSET document states β1 = 0.944(1.590−ν)/(1−ν) is what "Padmanabhan et al.
use" and separately that β1 = 1−2S1313 is "the exact Eshelby value." Both are
attributed to Eshelby (1957). This requires clarification:

  - The exact Eshelby computation (OSET §2.2) gives β1 = 1−2S1313 = 0.4456.
  - The formula β1 = 0.944(1.590−ν)/(1−ν) appears in the published papers as cited
    from Eshelby (1957), but it does not follow from the S1313 formula directly.
  - One plausible resolution: the papers' formula uses a DIFFERENT Eshelby tensor
    component (e.g., S for in-plane shear with a different orientation convention),
    or is from an older approximation (Argon 1979, Gittus 1975). This should be
    investigated and documented explicitly in OSET.
