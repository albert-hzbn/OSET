Near-random grain rotations, concomitant texture randomization
predominantly by grain boundary sliding as a result of superplastic
deformation even when one starts with a cold-rolled material of a highly
preferred grain orientation, insignificant grain growth compared with
the enormous specimen elongation, grain aspect ratio increasing from 1
to only ~ 1.2 even after several hundreds of percent elongation have
established that grain boundary sliding (GBS), accompanied by rather
limited GB migration (GBM), particularly compared with the extreme
tensile elongation, are important features that accompany this phe-
nomenon. Steady state, isotropic structural superplastic flow is reported
in FCC, BCC, HCP, and other crystal structures of metals and alloys,
ceramics, intermetallic compounds, metal-based and ceramic-based
composites, nanostructured materials, quasi-crystalline materials, bulk
metallic glasses, and geological materials. The nature of the atomic
bonds at the grain boundaries could be metallic, ionic, or covalent,
resulting in a wide variation of local atomic structures in the grain
boundaries (GBs). These observations clearly reveal that the generic
nature of this phenomenon is not likely to be explained by specific
interatomic interactions of a particular kind and/or purely geometric
grain boundary descriptions [3].

The major hurdles to be crossed if one persists with a geometric modeling approach are: Considering that the interatomic potential changes with
every alloy addition, impurity present etc. Here in the calculations it is assumed that the potential for Cu can be used for all Cu-alloys. It is unlikely to
be true although earlier, Frost and Ashby [52] assumed the same G value for all the alloys that have the same major constituent. While this may be
valid for the G and Оі values, whether it will be true for the activation energy and the other flow characteristics is not clear. If this is not true, the
B
calculations will have to be redone. There are already over 2000 material conditions of widely varying complexity in which superplastic flow has been
reported. This number will increase with time. Unless a generic technique to determine the interatomic potentials of all such alloys is developed, the
use geometric descriptors may be found wanting.
Therefore, it is better to develop a method for solving equations which is more traditional/is along an established route. In a future publication (the
calculations are nearly completed) a method of developing a physical model to arrive as the equations (already done in this paper) and solving the
unknown constants using available experimental results will be advocated and shown to be possible.
